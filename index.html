Novel hybrid cell detection in VisiumHD spatial transcriptomics

R  ·  Seurat  ·  SpaceRanger  ·  VisiumHD 2µm bins

Background

VisiumHD captures gene expression at as low as 2µm bin resolution, enabling subcellular analysis of tissue architecture. However, the scale and complexity of the data at this resolution — tens of millions of bins per sample — demands bespoke computational approaches that standard pipelines are not designed to handle.

This project involved the analysis of VisiumHD data from adrenal tissue, with the goal of characterising spatial gene expression patterns at high resolution.

The problem

During scRNA-seq analysis, a population of cells emerged that could not be assigned confidently to any known cell type, appearing to express markers from two previously distinct cell types. These hybrid cells were also seen at the standard 8µm bin level in VisiumHD analysis. The question was whether this represented a true biological hybrid population, a computational artefact, or contamination from adjacent tissue regions.

Approach

  • Spatial validation — examined the physical distribution of candidate hybrid bins relative to the tissue boundary between the two parent cell types
  • Transcriptional profiling — extracted differential gene expression between hybrid bins and both parent populations, identifying a unique transcriptional signature not reducible to either lineage
  • Co-expression analysis — confirmed simultaneous expression of lineage-defining markers within individual 2µm bins sitting clearly within the centre of H&E-demarked cells, ruling out doublet-style spatial contamination

Outcome

The analysis provided strong evidence for a genuine hybrid cell population with a distinct transcriptional identity. Importantly, the presence of these hybrid cells has potential clinical significance, details of which will be reported in the accompanying manuscript.

The pipeline developed for this project has since been adopted as a reusable internal workflow for VisiumHD analysis at the Genome Centre.

SenPred — machine learning classification of senescent cells from scRNA-seq

R  ·  Seurat  ·  scPred  ·  GEO public datasets

github.com/bethk-h/SenPred_HDF

Background

Cellular senescence — a state of stable cell cycle arrest accompanied by the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) — is a central hallmark of ageing and a driver of age-related tissue dysfunction. Despite its biological importance, identifying senescent cells reliably in vivo remains a major challenge: no single marker is definitive, and bulk approaches obscure cell-level heterogeneity.

The problem

Existing approaches relied either on bulk gene expression signatures or on a handful of canonical markers. There was no validated computational method to classify senescent cells at single-cell resolution from transcriptomic data, particularly across tissue contexts.

Approach

  • Training data — scRNA-seq of replicative senescent and early proliferative HDFs (GEO: GSE282425), three classes: Early Proliferative (EP), Early Senescent (ES), and Deep Senescent (DS)
  • Model training — cross-validated classification using scPred's SVM-based framework, optimised for class imbalance
  • Validation — applied to 5 independent external datasets: alternative in vitro senescence triggers (Teo et al.), three whole-skin in vivo studies (Tabib, Solé-Boldo, Ganier), and lung tissue from the Human Cell Atlas (Sikkema et al.)

Outcome

SenPred successfully classified senescent fibroblasts across all external datasets, demonstrating strong generalisation beyond the training tissue context. In whole-skin datasets, predicted senescence burden showed age-associated trends consistent with the biology — increasing deep senescent predictions in older donors — providing independent validation of the model's biological relevance.

The classifier represents the first validated single-cell tool for in vivo senescence detection from transcriptomics, with direct translational potential for identifying senescence burden in patient tissue.

Spatial transcriptomics of traumatic brain injury — linking tissue architecture to clinical outcomes

R  ·  Seurat  ·  SpaceRanger  ·  VisiumHD

Background

Traumatic brain injury produces complex, heterogeneous tissue responses that vary substantially between individuals and injury types. This project involved VisiumHD spatial transcriptomics of brain tissue from a prospective TBI cohort, with the goal of linking cell-type-level transcriptional landscapes to clinical and pathological metadata.

The problem

Standard spatial transcriptomics analysis treats each sample independently. This project required a multi-sample framework integrating rich clinical metadata — injury severity (GCS), CT imaging classification, injury mechanism, biopsy timing, anatomical site, and 6-month functional outcome (GOS-E) — with spatially-resolved cell type composition to ask whether transcriptional features of the tissue reflected or predicted clinical trajectories.

Approach

  • Sample processing — end-to-end SpaceRanger and Seurat pipeline across a multi-sample TBI cohort
  • Cell type annotation — unsupervised clustering and annotation of CNS-relevant populations including mature and stressed neuronal subtypes, multiple astrocyte states (reactive, excitotoxic, highly stressed), and oligodendrocyte subpopulations
  • Metadata integration — clinical variables mapped onto sample-level metadata including GCS severity, CT injury classification (DAI, ASDH, contusions/tSAH), TBI mechanism, biopsy type, and time post-injury
  • Proportion analysis — cell type proportions tested against clinical groups using Kruskal-Wallis tests (BH-corrected) and against continuous variables (GCS, biopsy time, GOS-E at 6 months) using Spearman correlation

Outcome

The analysis established a robust analytical framework for linking spatially-resolved cell type composition to clinical outcomes in TBI. The cohort in its current form is underpowered for definitive statistical conclusions — a common challenge in clinical spatial transcriptomics where sample numbers are constrained by tissue availability and consent — but the infrastructure is designed to scale directly as the cohort grows.

Notably, the annotation revealed a granular spectrum of glial stress states — distinguishing reactive, excitotoxic, and highly stressed astrocyte populations, and multiple functionally distinct oligodendrocyte subsets — that bulk or lower-resolution approaches would collapse into single categories.
